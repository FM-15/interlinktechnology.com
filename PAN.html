Personal Area Network (PAN)

A personal area network (PAN) links electronic devices within the close vicinity of a user. PANs vary in size from a few centimeters to several meters. One of the most prevalent instances of a PAN in the real world is the pairing of a Bluetooth earpiece and a smartphone. In addition to connecting laptops, printers, tablets, mouses, and keyboards, PANs may also connect additional computing devices. PANs transmit data between devices without requiring internet connectivity.

Advantages:

  • It is cost-effective because it doesn't require expensive installations or cable connections between devices, unlike conventional networks.
  • It is more secure than other networks because connecting endpoints require authorization from the authorized user before exchanging data.
  • It is more stable and dependable than larger networks because it operates independently of servers and functions within a limited range.
  • Devices can simultaneously connect to multiple other devices. For example, a smartphone may connect to several tablets and mobile phones at once.
  • PAN is adaptable and efficient for short ranges.

Disadvantages

  • PANs are slower than WAN, LAN, and MAN networks due to limited data transfer rates, especially with technologies like Bluetooth.
  • PANs have limited coverage, typically around 10 meters, and support only a restricted number of connected devices.
  • PANs, being wireless, emit hazardous radiation like microwave signals, which can adversely affect both humans and animals, leading to issues such as cerebral injury and heart problems.
